About

I am an economist at Villanova University, where I study the public finances and politics of state and local government. Much of what shapes daily life—the condition of the roads, the quality of the local schools, how quickly a 911 call brings help—is decided not in Washington but by municipalities, school districts, and states, and yet these governments receive far less attention from economists than they deserve. My work asks how the officials who run them respond to the incentives they face, and how those incentives, in turn, shape what citizens pay and what they receive.

My research spans public pensions, tax administration, public-sector unions, and wealth inequality. Two habits run through all of it: I build my own data when a question demands it—from a panel of every local pension plan in Pennsylvania to a global classification of billionaires by the source of their wealth—and I rely on natural experiments and careful research design to reach answers that are causal, concrete, and useful to those who make policy.

Research

My agenda comprises three established strands—public-sector labor markets, the administration and enforcement of taxes, and the causes and consequences of wealth inequality—along with a smaller body of work on regulation.

The political economy of public-sector labor markets

How do the electoral incentives of officials shape what public employees are promised, whether those promises are funded, and the size and composition of the public workforce? I show that more politically competitive jurisdictions offer more generous and less well-funded public pensions, and even choose discount-rate assumptions that understate their liabilities—effects that vanish for pension plans run by appointed boards insulated from voters. Since tenure, I have opened a new line on public-sector unions, providing the first administrative-data assessment of the Supreme Court's 2018 decision in Janus v. AFSCME.

The administration and enforcement of taxes

How a government collects a tax—not merely the rate it sets—is often decisive for revenue, compliance, and the size of the state. Exploiting the staggered adoption of income-tax withholding by U.S. states and Pennsylvania's Act 32, I find that withholding and third-party reporting produce large, permanent increases in collections, and I trace the channels through which compliance rises. Related work examines the politics of IRS enforcement and the incentives of elected tax collectors.

Wealth inequality, political connections, growth, and democracy

Using a measure of wealth concentration built from the Forbes billionaire lists, I find that the source of great wealth matters far more than its magnitude: it is wealth tied to political connections, rather than wealth earned in the market, that is associated with slower growth and weaker democracy. This work has been discussed in Forbes, The Guardian, and The Washington Post.
